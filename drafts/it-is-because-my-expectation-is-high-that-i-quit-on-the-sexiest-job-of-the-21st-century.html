It is because my expectation is high that I quit on the sexiest job of the 21st century

Quitting the sexiest job of the 21st century [1] and jumping head-first into a 3-person startup pre-revenue (read: no salary) is a risk-seeking move. Particularly when Big Data and Data Science are gaining tractions these days. Take a look at the Data Science London meetup group [2]. Just over a year ago when the meetup started, I recall seeing about 50 people in the audience. These days they are consistently booked full with 150+ and occasionally up to 300+ attendees limited only by the venue capacity. Wouldn't it be smarter for me to stay in that career path to ride the wave? Perhaps. Perhaps not. I wouldn't know until another 10 years. What I do want to discuss in this article is a mental exercise to rationalise such a career move by comparing it to financial investing.

Taking a step back, investing is defined as trading one asset for another to make a profit. Apply that thinking of trading one thing for another is an investment to trading your time and expertise in exchange for money. If you can agree with that, then employment is a form of investment. And if that is so, then we can apply Modern Portfolio Theory and Efficient Market Hypothesis to assess career choices like they do with stocks.

You're doing your job because you feel that your investment return (i.e. salary) is a good compensation for your investment of time, effort, and expertise.

Salaried job is like blue chips. Startup is like high growth stocks.

Conversely, staying my course as an employed data scientist would be a risk averse activity. I would have been preferring a more certain expected payoff versus an uncertain payoff. In an efficient market, the expected reward of a certain payoff is likely to be less than the reward of an uncertain payoff because you'd be taking on more

"You know, it's a bull market" [3]
